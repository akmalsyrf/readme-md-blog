# The History of Kromo Inggil and Its Relationship with Sanskrit

The Javanese language has a complex system of speech levels, and at its pinnacle is **Kromo Inggil** (or Krama Inggil)—the most refined and respectful level of speech. Interestingly, much of Kromo Inggil's vocabulary comes from Sanskrit, the classical Indian language that once served as a lingua franca in South and Southeast Asia. How did this happen? And why did Java become a "preserver" of Sanskrit in the archipelago?

## Origins: Sanskrit in the Archipelago

Sanskrit began entering the Indonesian archipelago around the 4th to 5th century CE, brought by traders, priests, and scholars from India. During this period, Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms such as Tarumanagara, Kutai, and later Majapahit used Sanskrit as the official language for:

- **Royal inscriptions and documents**: Most ancient inscriptions were written in Sanskrit
- **Literature and religious texts**: Hindu and Buddhist sacred texts were translated or written in Sanskrit
- **Ceremonies and rituals**: Religious mantras were recited in Sanskrit

Sanskrit became a symbol of **prestige, wisdom, and power**. Using Sanskrit vocabulary indicated a high level of education and social status.

## Why Did Kromo Inggil Absorb Sanskrit?

The Javanese language has a very refined system of **undha-usuk** (speech levels). There are three main levels:

1. **Ngoko**: Everyday language, familiar, for equals or inferiors
2. **Madya**: Middle language, semi-formal
3. **Krama/Kromo Inggil**: Refined language, to honor elders or those of higher status

As this system developed, Javanese nobles and scholars needed different vocabulary to show **respect and social distance**. Sanskrit, already known as a "high" and "sacred" language, became the perfect source.

### Examples of Transformation

- **Ngoko**: "mangan" (eat) → **Kromo Inggil**: "dhahar" (from Sanskrit: _dahara_)
- **Ngoko**: "turu" (sleep) → **Kromo Inggil**: "sare" (from Sanskrit: _shayana_)
- **Ngoko**: "lunga" (go) → **Kromo Inggil**: "tindak" (from Sanskrit: _tadaga_)
- **Ngoko**: "wong" (person) → **Kromo Inggil**: "tiyang" (from Sanskrit: _tyaga_)

## Java as a "Preserver" of Sanskrit

This is an interesting historical irony: **Sanskrit died in India, but lived on in Java**.

### The Death of Sanskrit in India

In India itself, Sanskrit began to decline from the 13th century CE due to:

1. **Muslim invasions**: Muslim kingdoms preferred Persian and Urdu
2. **Rise of regional languages**: Hindi, Bengali, Tamil, and other regional languages replaced Sanskrit
3. **Modernization**: English became the language of education and administration

By the 19th century, Sanskrit had become a "dead" language—only studied by scholars, no longer used in daily conversation.

### The Life of Sanskrit in Java

Meanwhile, in Java, Sanskrit became **integrated** into daily language through Kromo Inggil:

1. **Active use**: Sanskrit vocabulary was used in formal conversation, not just in ancient texts
2. **Natural evolution**: Sanskrit words underwent Javanese phonetic adaptation (e.g., _shayana_ → _sare_)
3. **Semantic expansion**: Some Sanskrit words acquired new meanings in Javanese context
4. **Preservation**: Many Sanskrit words lost in India are still used in Java

### Examples of Words "Alive" in Java

- **"Dharma"**: In modern India, this word is rarely used in conversation. In Java, "dharma" is still used in formal and religious contexts.
- **"Karma"**: Like dharma, "karma" is more alive in Javanese Kromo Inggil than in modern India.
- **"Sare"** (sleep): From _shayana_, this word is still actively used in Kromo Inggil, while in India it has become archaic.

## Factors That Made This Happen

### 1. Strong Social Stratification System

Java has a very strict social hierarchy system. Kromo Inggil became a tool to **maintain and assert** this hierarchy. Sanskrit provided vocabulary that was "high enough" for this purpose.

### 2. Strong Literary Tradition

Java has a very rich literary tradition. Ancient texts such as _Ramayana_ and _Mahabharata_ in Javanese versions continue to be studied and performed. This keeps Sanskrit vocabulary relevant.

### 3. Religion and Philosophy

Although the majority of Javanese are now Muslim, many Hindu-Buddhist concepts remain alive in Javanese culture. These concepts are often expressed with Sanskrit vocabulary.

### 4. Traditional Education System

Traditional Javanese pesantren (Islamic boarding schools) and padepokan (traditional schools) teach ancient texts full of Sanskrit vocabulary. This ensures knowledge transmission across generations.

## Cultural and Linguistic Impact

### Positive

1. **Linguistic richness**: Javanese became one of the languages with the richest vocabulary in the world
2. **Cultural preservation**: Many ancient concepts and values remain alive through language
3. **Cultural identity**: Kromo Inggil became a marker of unique Javanese cultural identity

### Challenges

1. **Complexity**: The undha-usuk system makes Javanese difficult to learn
2. **Declining use**: Young Javanese generations increasingly rarely use Kromo Inggil
3. **Globalization**: Indonesian and English are displacing the use of regional languages

## Conclusion

Javanese Kromo Inggil is an interesting example of how **languages can cross geographical and temporal boundaries**. Sanskrit, which died in its homeland, found new life in Java through a sophisticated undha-usuk system.

This relationship is not merely passive borrowing, but a **creative adaptation** that made Sanskrit an integral part of Javanese linguistic identity. Java did not just "borrow" Sanskrit—Java **revived** Sanskrit in a new and functional form.

In this modern era, when Kromo Inggil is beginning to be threatened with extinction, we are losing not just a speech level, but also a **living bridge** that connects us to the ancient civilization that once flourished in the archipelago.
