# Book Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind

"Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind" is a book by Yuval Noah Harari, an Israeli historian. This book tells the history of humankind from the stone age to the modern era, with a provocative approach that challenges conventional thinking about history.

## Main Thesis

Harari argues that **Homo sapiens** succeeded in dominating the world not because of physical or intellectual individual superiority, but because of the unique ability to **collaborate on a large scale** through shared fiction.

## Three Great Revolutions

Harari divides human history into three great revolutions:

### 1. Cognitive Revolution (around 70,000 years ago)

**What happened:**

- Homo sapiens developed complex language abilities
- Ability to talk about things that don't exist (fiction)
- Ability to collaborate in large groups

**Why it's important:**

- Previously, humans could only collaborate in small groups (maximum 150 people)
- After the Cognitive Revolution, humans could collaborate with thousands or even millions of people
- This enabled the formation of tribes, kingdoms, and civilizations

**Examples of shared fiction:**

- **Religion**: Gods, spirits, heaven, hell
- **Money**: Agreed-upon value
- **State**: Abstract entity that regulates life
- **Company**: Legal entity that can own assets

### 2. Agricultural Revolution (around 12,000 years ago)

**What happened:**

- Humans began planting crops and raising animals
- Transition from nomadic to settled lifestyle
- Dramatic population growth

**Agricultural Paradox:**

Harari presents a controversial perspective: **The Agricultural Revolution may have been the biggest mistake in human history**.

**Arguments:**

- **Before agriculture**: Humans lived as hunter-gatherers
  - Worked 3-4 hours per day
  - Diverse and healthy diet
  - Lots of leisure time
  - No mass infectious diseases

- **After agriculture**: Humans lived as farmers
  - Worked 12-14 hours per day
  - Monotonous diet (mainly wheat)
  - Little leisure time
  - Mass infectious diseases
  - Large social inequality

**Why did humans continue?**

- **Trap**: Once started, hard to go back
- **Population**: Agriculture enabled larger population, making return to old lifestyle impossible
- **Elite**: Elite who benefited from agriculture maintained it

### 3. Scientific Revolution (around 500 years ago)

**What happened:**

- Humans acknowledged their ignorance
- Developed scientific method
- Massive exploration and discovery

**Key difference:**

Before the Scientific Revolution, humans believed all knowledge already existed in ancient texts. After the Scientific Revolution, humans acknowledged that **much is still unknown** and must be discovered.

**Supporting factors:**

- **Capitalism**: Economic system that encourages innovation
- **Imperialism**: Exploration to find new resources
- **Attitude toward ignorance**: Accepting that we don't know everything

## Important Concepts

### 1. Shared Fiction

Shared fiction is a story agreed upon by many people, even though it doesn't exist in physical reality:

- **Money**: A piece of paper has no intrinsic value, but we all agree it's valuable
- **Human rights**: Don't exist in natural law, but we all agree humans have rights
- **Company**: Doesn't exist physically, but we all treat it as a real entity

Shared fiction enables mass collaboration.

### 2. Imperialism and Capitalism

Harari explains how European imperialism and capitalism reinforced each other:

- **Capitalism** needs continuous expansion
- **Imperialism** provides new markets and resources
- **Science** provides technology for exploration and conquest

### 3. Happiness

Harari questions whether modern humans are happier than their ancestors:

- **Living standards**: Clearly better
- **Happiness**: Unclear if happier
- **Depression and anxiety**: Mental health problems increasing

### 4. The Future

Harari discusses possible futures:

- **Bioengineering**: Biological engineering to enhance humans
- **Cyborg**: Humans integrated with technology
- **Artificial consciousness**: AI with consciousness

## Criticism and Controversy

This book received much praise but also criticism:

### Positive Criticism

- **Provocative**: Forces readers to rethink their assumptions
- **Comprehensive**: Covers human history broadly
- **Accessible**: Written in an easily understandable way

### Negative Criticism

- **Too deterministic**: Too emphasizes single factor (shared fiction)
- **Oversimplification**: Simplifies complex history
- **Bias**: Very Western/European perspective
- **Speculative**: Many claims difficult to prove
- **Agricultural controversy**: Argument that agriculture is a "mistake" is very controversial

## Lessons to Learn

### 1. Power of Narrative

Narratives and stories we believe have great power to shape reality.

### 2. Complexity of History

History is not linear. Developments that seem "positive" may have unexpected negative consequences.

### 3. Role of Collaboration

Large-scale collaboration is the key to human success, not individual superiority.

### 4. Questions about Progress

Does "progress" always mean improvement? Harari forces us to question this assumption.

## Conclusion

"Sapiens" is a provocative and challenging book. Harari forces us to:

- **Question assumptions**: About history, progress, and happiness
- **Understand narrative power**: How stories shape reality
- **See big patterns**: Beyond details to see historical patterns
- **Think critically**: About the future and our choices

Although not all of Harari's arguments are universally accepted, this book successfully sparks important discussions about human history and its future.

As Harari says: "We are the only animals that can collaborate flexibly in large numbers. And we do it by believing in shared fiction."
