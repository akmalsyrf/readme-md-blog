# History of Computer Terms Taken from Old Devices

The computer world is full of terms familiar to our ears: "desktop", "folder", "file", "window", "mouse". But did you know that many of these terms come from devices and technologies that are no longer used? Let's explore the interesting history behind these terms.

## Desktop: From Work Desk to Computer Screen

**Desktop** is a very literal term. In the era before computers, office workers had physical work desks where they placed documents, folders, and writing tools. When graphical interfaces were first developed, designers used this desk metaphor to make computers more familiar.

Xerox PARC, which developed the first graphical interface in the 1970s, used the "desktop" concept to describe the computer screen as a virtual work desk. This concept was then adopted by Apple with Lisa and Macintosh, and eventually became an industry standard.

## Folder and File: Legacy of Paper Filing Systems

**Folder** and **file** are direct legacies from paper document storage systems. In old offices, documents were stored in paper folders that were then arranged in filing cabinets.

When operating systems first needed a way to organize data, designers used this already familiar metaphor:

- **File**: Individual document (like a sheet of paper)
- **Folder**: Container to organize files (like a paper folder)
- **Directory**: Hierarchical folder system (like a filing cabinet)

Even the folder icon in modern operating systems still resembles a paper folder with a tab on top!

## Window: From Physical Window to Digital Window

**Window** is a very powerful metaphor. In the physical world, windows allow us to look into or out of a room. In the digital world, "window" allows us to look into an application or document.

The window concept was first introduced in Xerox Alto in 1973, and became popular with Apple Macintosh and Microsoft Windows. This metaphor was very successful because:

- Easy to understand: Everyone knows what a window is
- Intuitive: Opening/closing a window is like opening/closing a physical window
- Flexible: Can stack, move, and resize

## Mouse: From Animal to Input Device

**Mouse** gets its name because its shape and cable resemble a mouse. Douglas Engelbart, creator of the first mouse in the 1960s, called it that because the cable connected to the device resembled a mouse's tail.

Although modern mice are wireless, the name "mouse" persists. In fact, some languages have literal translations: in German it's called "Maus", in French "souris".

## Clipboard: From Clip Board to Temporary Memory

**Clipboard** is a physical device used to hold papers while writing. In the computer world, clipboard is a temporary memory area that stores copied or cut data.

This metaphor is very apt because:

- Physical clipboard holds documents temporarily before moving them
- Digital clipboard holds data temporarily before pasting
- Both are temporary (physical clipboard is emptied, digital clipboard is overwritten)

## Trash/Recycle Bin: From Garbage to File Deletion

**Trash** or **Recycle Bin** is a very intuitive metaphor. In the physical world, we throw items into the trash. In the digital world, we "throw" files into trash.

Interestingly, the "recycle bin" concept is more modern and reflects the ability to "restore" deleted files—something impossible with traditional physical trash.

## Icon: From Religious Symbol to Digital Symbol

**Icon** comes from the Greek word "eikōn" meaning image or representation. In religious context, icons are sacred images. In computer context, icons are visual representations of files, folders, or applications.

The use of the term "icon" began with the first graphical interfaces, and remains used today because of its ability to convey meaning visually and quickly.

## Bug: From Real Insect to Program Error

The term **bug** has a very interesting history. In 1947, Grace Hopper found a moth trapped inside the Harvard Mark II computer, causing an error. Since then, the term "bug" has been used to describe program errors.

Although the term "bug" was used before (even by Thomas Edison), Grace Hopper's incident made this term popular in the computer world.

## Browser: From Explorer to Web Explorer

**Browser** comes from the word "browse" which means to explore or look around. In the pre-internet era, people "browsed" through catalogs or stores. In the digital era, browsers allow us to "browse" through the World Wide Web.

## Conclusion

The computer terms we use daily are bridges between the physical and digital worlds. They help us understand new technology by using concepts already familiar from the real world.

However, there's an irony here: young generations who grew up with digital technology may never have seen paper folders, physical desktops, or even mice with cables. For them, these terms may feel abstract, even though they still use them.

This shows how powerful metaphors are in shaping how we think and interact with technology. And although the original physical devices may no longer be used, their names live on in the digital world.
